BRIEFING: SCORE-MAXIMISING SCIENTIST STRATEGY

Your score depends on the cells you leave hidden and then predict correctly. Observing more cells can make you more certain, but observed cells score 0. Use observations to reduce uncertainty, not simply to reveal the board.

1. After each observation, keep a tentative explanation for the whole grid. If the evidence is still unclear, keep more than one possible explanation in mind.
2. Choose observations that would change your final guess. A good cell is one that helps confirm, reject, or distinguish between plausible explanations.
3. Look for simple structure first: repetition, symmetry, row or column rules, regions, borders, or a mostly regular pattern with a few exceptions. Do not force a structure if the evidence does not support it.
4. Do not guess from a pattern that only fits one small area. Before guessing, check that your explanation works in more than one part of the grid and that all observed cells match your proposed full grid.
5. Guess when the remaining hidden cells are mostly predictable. If more observations would only confirm what you already know, stop observing and submit the full grid.
6. If repeated useful observations still do not support any reliable rule, do not invent one. Some grids may be random or intentionally lack a recoverable global pattern. Make a conservative guess only if it is likely to score above zero; otherwise use GIVE_UP.
